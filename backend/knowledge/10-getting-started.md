# Getting Started as a New Trustee

## So You're a Trustee — Now What?

Being named a trustee is a significant responsibility. Whether you're a family member stepping in to help or a professional taking on a new client, the first 30 days set the tone for your entire administration. Here's what you need to do to get started on the right foot.

## First 30 Days: Your Action Plan

### Days 1–7: Get Oriented

**Read the trust document.** This is the single most important thing you can do. The trust document tells you what you can and cannot do, who the beneficiaries are, what distribution standards apply, and how the trust should be administered. Read it carefully, and preferably more than once.

**Identify the key players.** Make a list of:
- All beneficiaries and their contact information
- Co-trustees, if any
- The attorney who drafted the trust
- Any financial advisors, CPAs, or other professionals already involved
- The successor trustee named in the document

**Secure the assets.** Take immediate steps to protect trust property. This may include:
- Changing locks on real property
- Notifying banks and financial institutions of your authority
- Obtaining insurance if needed
- Collecting and safeguarding valuables

### Days 8–14: Build Your Foundation

**Create a Schedule A.** Inventory every asset the trust holds. Include descriptions, values, account numbers, and how each asset is titled. Don't skip this — it's your starting point and your protection. See the Schedule A guide for detailed instructions.

**Open a trust bank account.** You'll need a separate account in the name of the trust using the trust's tax identification number. Do not commingle trust funds with your personal accounts. This is one of the most common and most serious mistakes new trustees make.

**Set up your record-keeping system.** Whether you use TrustOffice, a spreadsheet, or a filing cabinet, you need a system for tracking:
- Income and expenses
- Distributions
- Investment decisions
- Communications with beneficiaries
- Tax documents

**Get an EIN.** If the trust doesn't already have an Employer Identification Number, apply for one from the IRS. You'll need it to open bank accounts and file tax returns.

### Days 15–21: Establish Communication

**Notify the beneficiaries.** Many states require you to notify beneficiaries when a trust becomes irrevocable or when you take over as trustee. Even where it's not required, it's good practice. A brief, professional letter introducing yourself and explaining your role can prevent misunderstandings later.

**Meet with professionals.** Start building your advisory team:
- **A trust attorney** to advise on your duties and state-specific requirements
- **A CPA or tax professional** experienced in fiduciary tax matters
- **A financial advisor** if the trust has significant investment assets

You don't have to go it alone. In fact, consulting with professionals is evidence of prudent administration.

**Set up your compliance calendar.** Identify important deadlines:
- Tax filing dates (federal and state)
- Required notice deadlines
- Accounting due dates
- Insurance renewal dates
- Recurring distribution dates

### Days 22–30: Start Administering

**Review the investments.** Evaluate whether the current investment strategy aligns with the trust's purposes and the beneficiaries' needs. Document your assessment using Guided Minutes.

**Address any immediate needs.** If a beneficiary has an urgent distribution request or a pressing expense, evaluate it promptly. Don't let requests sit unanswered.

**Draft your first minutes.** Use Guided Minutes to document your initial review of the trust, the decisions you've made in these first 30 days, and your plan going forward.

**Set a schedule.** Plan for regular reviews — quarterly at minimum, monthly for active trusts. Put these on your calendar now so they don't fall through the cracks.

## Common Pitfalls for New Trustees

### Commingling Funds
Never mix trust money with your own. Keep a separate trust bank account and use it exclusively for trust transactions. Commingling is one of the fastest ways to invite legal trouble.

### Acting Without Reading the Trust
Every trust is different. Before making any decision, confirm that the trust document gives you the authority to do what you're planning. If you're unsure, consult an attorney.

### Ignoring Beneficiaries
Silence breeds suspicion. Even if you have nothing new to report, regular communication shows beneficiaries that you're paying attention and taking your role seriously.

### Delaying Decisions
Beneficiaries and courts expect timely administration. If a distribution request takes months to evaluate, or if you let filings go overdue, it looks like neglect — even if your eventual decision is correct.

### Skipping Documentation
The road to trouble is paved with good intentions and no paperwork. Document every decision, every communication, and every transaction. If it isn't written down, it didn't happen.

### Going It Alone
You're not expected to be an expert in investing, taxes, and law simultaneously. Hiring qualified professionals is not a sign of weakness — it's evidence of prudence. The cost of professional advice is typically a trust expense, not a personal one.
