# Trust Distributions

## Understanding Trust Distributions

A trust distribution is any payment or transfer of assets from the trust to a beneficiary. Distributions are one of the trustee's most important — and most scrutinized — responsibilities. Getting them right means following the trust document, documenting your reasoning, and understanding the tax implications.

## Types of Distributions

### HEMS Distributions

When a trust uses a HEMS standard (Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support), the trustee has discretion to make distributions that fall within those four categories. The trustee evaluates each request and decides whether it qualifies and whether it's appropriate given the beneficiary's circumstances.

Key characteristics of HEMS distributions:

- The trust document gives the trustee *discretion*, not a mandate
- The trustee should consider the beneficiary's other resources
- Each distribution should fit clearly within one of the four HEMS categories
- Documentation should explain how the distribution qualifies

### Discretionary Distributions

Some trusts give the trustee broad discretion to make distributions for a beneficiary's "best interest," "comfort," or general welfare — without tying decisions to specific categories like HEMS. This gives the trustee more flexibility but also more responsibility.

With discretionary distributions:

- The trustee has wide latitude, but decisions must still be reasonable
- The trustee should consider the needs of all beneficiaries, not just the one requesting funds
- Documentation is even more important because the standard is more subjective
- The trustee should be able to articulate a clear rationale for every distribution

### Mandatory Distributions

Some trusts require the trustee to make specific distributions — for example, "distribute all net income annually" or "pay $5,000 per month to the beneficiary." These are not discretionary. The trustee must make these distributions as written, regardless of personal opinion about whether they're wise.

### Principal vs. Income Distributions

Trusts often distinguish between distributing **income** (interest, dividends, rent) and **principal** (the trust's core assets). Some trusts allow income distributions more freely than principal distributions. Understanding this distinction is important because:

- Beneficiaries may have different rights to income vs. principal
- Tax treatment differs between the two
- The trust accounting rules (sometimes following the Uniform Principal and Income Act) determine what counts as income vs. principal

## Documentation Requirements

Every distribution — approved or denied — should be documented. Good records include:

1. **The request:** Who asked, what they asked for, and when
2. **The purpose:** What the distribution is for and which standard it falls under
3. **Your analysis:** How you evaluated the request, what factors you considered
4. **The decision:** Approved, denied, or approved for a modified amount
5. **The rationale:** Why you made this decision
6. **The beneficiary's circumstances:** Their income, assets, and other relevant context
7. **The payment:** Amount, date, and how funds were transferred

If you deny a request, document that too. A pattern of thoughtful denials — with clear reasoning — can be just as important as approved distributions in showing you're managing the trust responsibly.

## Notifying Beneficiaries

When you make a distribution, it's good practice to:

- **Confirm the purpose** with the beneficiary in writing before sending funds
- **Provide a written record** of the distribution, including the amount and category
- **Include distribution information** in regular accountings to all beneficiaries
- **Be transparent** — other beneficiaries may have a right to know about distributions, depending on state law and the trust terms

For denied requests, a brief written explanation can help maintain a positive relationship and reduce the likelihood of disputes.

## Tax Implications

Distributions carry tax consequences for both the trust and the beneficiary:

- **Income distributed to beneficiaries** is generally taxed to the beneficiary, not the trust. The trust gets a deduction for distributions of distributable net income (DNI).
- **Principal distributions** are typically not taxable to the beneficiary, but they reduce the trust's assets and may have capital gains implications if the trust sells assets to fund the distribution.
- **The trust's marginal tax rates** are compressed — meaning the trust pays high rates on relatively low amounts of retained income. Strategic distributions can reduce the overall tax burden.

This is general information only. Trustees should work with a tax professional to understand the specific tax consequences of distributions for their trust.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Playing favorites:** Treating one beneficiary differently without justification
- **Missing the standard:** Making a distribution that doesn't fit the trust's distribution language
- **Poor documentation:** Approving distributions without writing down your reasoning
- **Ignoring tax consequences:** Not considering how distributions affect the trust and beneficiaries' taxes
- **Delayed decisions:** Letting distribution requests sit unanswered, which can frustrate beneficiaries and create liability
