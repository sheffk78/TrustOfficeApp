# Trustee Fiduciary Duties

## What Does Fiduciary Mean?

A fiduciary is someone legally obligated to act in the best interest of another person. As a trustee, you are a fiduciary — meaning you must put the beneficiaries' interests ahead of your own. This isn't just a moral standard; it's a legal one. Courts take fiduciary duties seriously, and violating them can result in personal liability.

## The Four Core Duties

### Duty of Loyalty

The duty of loyalty requires you to act solely in the beneficiaries' interests. You must avoid **self-dealing** — using your position as trustee to benefit yourself at the expense of the trust.

This means:

- You cannot buy trust assets for yourself at a discount
- You cannot sell your own assets to the trust at a premium
- You cannot steer trust business to companies you own or have a stake in
- You cannot borrow from the trust or loan trust funds to yourself

Even the *appearance* of a conflict can be a problem. If you have a personal interest that might conflict with your duty, disclose it immediately and consider whether you should step aside from that particular decision.

### Duty of Prudence

The duty of prudence requires you to manage trust assets with reasonable care, skill, and caution. The modern standard comes from the **Uniform Prudent Investor Act (UPIA)**, which emphasizes:

- **Total portfolio approach:** Don't evaluate investments in isolation. Look at how each asset contributes to the overall trust portfolio.
- **Risk and return:** Consider both. A completely risk-free strategy that doesn't keep up with inflation may violate this duty just as much as a reckless one.
- **Diversification:** Unless the trust document says otherwise, diversify investments to reduce risk.
- **Costs:** Be mindful of fees, transaction costs, and tax consequences.
- **Regular review:** Monitor investments and trust performance on an ongoing basis, not just when problems arise.

You're not expected to be a financial genius, but you are expected to act as a reasonably prudent person would with their own assets — and in many cases, to seek professional advice when you lack expertise.

### Duty of Impartiality

If a trust has multiple beneficiaries, you owe a duty to **all of them** — not just the one who calls the most or seems the neediest.

Common tension points include:

- **Current vs. remainder beneficiaries:** Income beneficiaries want high current income; remainder beneficiaries want the trust principal to grow. You must balance both interests.
- **Competing needs:** One beneficiary may need distributions for medical expenses while another wants funds for education. You must weigh each request fairly.
- **Investment strategy:** Aggressive growth may favor remainder beneficiaries, while conservative income investments favor current beneficiaries.

The trust document often provides guidance on how to balance these interests, and you should follow that guidance. Where it's silent, you must use your best judgment to treat all beneficiaries fairly.

### Duty to Account

You must keep beneficiaries reasonably informed about the trust and its administration. This includes:

- **Providing accountings:** Regular reports showing trust assets, income, expenses, and distributions
- **Responding to requests:** Answering reasonable questions from beneficiaries about trust matters
- **Disclosing material information:** Sharing important developments that affect the trust or the beneficiaries' interests
- **Keeping records:** Maintaining organized, detailed records of every financial transaction and decision

Good accounting isn't just about compliance — it's your best protection. Detailed records demonstrate that you've fulfilled your duties and make it much harder for anyone to claim you've mismanaged the trust.

## Why These Duties Matter

Violating fiduciary duties can result in:

- **Personal liability** for losses to the trust
- **Removal** as trustee by a court
- **Surcharge** — a court order requiring you to repay the trust from your own funds
- **Legal fees** that come out of your pocket, not the trust's

Even well-intentioned mistakes can create problems. The key is to document your decisions, seek professional advice when needed, and always ask yourself: *"Am I acting in the beneficiaries' best interest, or am I serving my own convenience?"*
