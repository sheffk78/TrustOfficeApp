# State Trust Compliance

## Why State Law Matters

Trusts are governed primarily by state law, not federal law. That means the rules about how a trust should be administered, what filings are required, and what rights beneficiaries have can vary significantly depending on which state the trust is governed by. Understanding your compliance obligations is essential to avoiding legal trouble and protecting both yourself and the trust.

The trust document itself usually specifies which state's law applies. If it doesn't, the state where the trust is administered — typically where the trustee lives or where the trust's primary assets are located — will generally govern.

## Common State Filing Requirements

### Trust Registration

Some states require trusts to be registered with a court or state agency. This is often a simple filing that provides basic information about the trust, the trustee, and the beneficiaries. Not all states require this, and the rules have been relaxed in many jurisdictions, but it's worth checking.

### Annual Accountings

Many states give beneficiaries the right to receive periodic accountings from the trustee. In some states, this is mandatory — the trustee must provide an accounting at regular intervals, such as annually. In others, the trustee only needs to account if a beneficiary requests it.

An accounting typically includes:

- A list of all trust assets and their values
- All income received during the period
- All expenses and distributions made
- Gains and losses on investments

### Income Tax Filings

Most states with an income tax require trusts to file state tax returns, similar to the federal Form 1041. If the trust earns income, has a connection to the state (such as a trustee or beneficiary residing there), or holds property in the state, a state filing may be required. Some trusts may need to file in multiple states.

### Property Tax and Transfer Requirements

Real property held in a trust may still be subject to state and local property taxes. Trustees should ensure property taxes are paid and that ownership records reflect the trust correctly. Some states also require specific documentation when transferring real property into or out of a trust.

## Notice Requirements

Many states have specific rules about when and how trustees must notify beneficiaries about trust matters:

### Initial Notice

When a trust becomes irrevocable — typically when the person who created it dies — many states require the trustee to notify all qualified beneficiaries within a certain time frame. This notice usually includes:

- The existence of the trust
- The identity of the trustee
- The beneficiary's right to request copies of the trust document

### Ongoing Notices

Some states require trustees to provide periodic notices about trust administration, including accountings and material changes to the trust. Even where not required, providing regular updates is a best practice that reduces the likelihood of disputes.

### Notice of Changes

If the trustee changes, if the trust is amended, or if there's a significant change in how the trust is administered, beneficiaries may need to be notified.

## Jurisdiction Considerations

Several factors can determine which state has jurisdiction over a trust:

- **The trust document:** Most well-drafted trusts specify a governing state.
- **Trustee's residence:** The state where the trustee lives often has jurisdiction.
- **Asset location:** Real property is governed by the state where it's located.
- **Beneficiary residence:** Some states claim jurisdiction based on beneficiary residency.
- **Trust administration:** The state where the trust is primarily administered.

If a trust has connections to multiple states, compliance can get complex. Each state may have different rules about taxation, notice, and filing, and you may need to satisfy requirements in more than one state.

## Practical Steps for Trustees

1. **Identify the governing state** as specified in the trust document.
2. **Research that state's requirements** for trust registration, notices, and accountings.
3. **Check for multi-state obligations** if the trust has assets or connections in other states.
4. **Set up a compliance calendar** with deadlines for filings, notices, and accountings.
5. **Consult a local attorney** who practices trust law in the governing state to confirm you've covered all requirements.
6. **Document your compliance efforts** — keep copies of everything you file and every notice you send.
