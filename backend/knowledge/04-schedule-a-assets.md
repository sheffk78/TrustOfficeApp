# Schedule A: Trust Assets

## What Is Schedule A?

Schedule A is a detailed inventory of all assets held by a trust. Think of it as the trust's complete property list — a snapshot of everything the trust owns at a given point in time. For trustees, Schedule A is one of the most important documents in trust administration because it establishes what you're responsible for managing.

When you take over as trustee, creating or reviewing Schedule A should be one of your very first tasks. It's the foundation for everything else you'll do: investing, distributing, accounting, and reporting.

## What Assets Go on Schedule A

Schedule A should include every asset the trust holds. Common categories include:

### Real Estate
- Primary residences
- Vacation or rental properties
- Vacant land
- Commercial properties
- Include addresses, legal descriptions, and estimated values

### Financial Accounts
- Checking and savings accounts
- Brokerage and investment accounts
- Money market accounts
- Certificates of deposit
- Include account numbers, institutions, and balances

### Investment Holdings
- Stocks and bonds
- Mutual funds and ETFs
- Partnership interests
- Other securities
- Include number of shares, cost basis, and current values

### Retirement Accounts
- IRAs
- 401(k)s
- Other retirement assets (if payable to or held by the trust)

### Business Interests
- Ownership in LLCs, corporations, or partnerships
- Sole proprietorships
- Include percentage ownership and valuation

### Personal Property
- Vehicles (cars, boats, aircraft)
- Jewelry and valuables
- Art and collectibles
- Household furnishings
- Include descriptions and appraised values where available

### Other Assets
- Life insurance policies (if the trust is owner or beneficiary)
- Intellectual property or royalties
- Loans receivable
- Digital assets

## How to Log Assets

When creating or updating Schedule A, follow these principles:

1. **Be specific.** Don't write "bank account." Write "Chase Bank checking account ending in 4521, balance $23,450 as of January 15, 2026."

2. **Include valuations.** State the fair market value of each asset as of a specific date. For assets that fluctuate (like stocks), note the date of the valuation.

3. **Note the source.** Indicate how you determined each value — was it a bank statement, a professional appraisal, an online quote, or a reasonable estimate?

4. **Track cost basis.** Record the original purchase price or cost basis where available. This is critical for tax purposes when assets are later sold.

5. **Update regularly.** Schedule A is a living document. Revisit it when assets are bought, sold, or change significantly in value.

## Why Schedule A Matters

Schedule A serves several critical purposes in trust administration:

- **Accountability:** It establishes a clear baseline of what the trust owned when you took over, protecting you from claims that assets are missing.
- **Decision-making:** You can't manage what you don't know about. A complete inventory is essential for investment decisions and distribution planning.
- **Tax reporting:** Accurate asset records are necessary for filing Form 1041 and calculating gains or losses.
- **Beneficiary communication:** Beneficiaries have a right to know what the trust holds. Schedule A forms the basis of your accounting to them.
- **Transition:** If you step down as trustee, Schedule A ensures a smooth handoff to your successor.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Missing assets:** Forgetting to include a small account, a personal item, or a digital asset can create gaps in your records.
- **Stale valuations:** Using outdated values misrepresents the trust's financial position.
- **Vague descriptions:** "Some stocks" or "a car" don't hold up in an accounting or audit.
- **No cost basis:** Without basis information, calculating capital gains at sale time becomes difficult and may result in higher taxes.
- **Infrequent updates:** Schedule A that's years out of date is unreliable and potentially misleading.
