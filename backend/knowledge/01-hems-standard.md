# HEMS Distribution Standard

## What Is HEMS?

HEMS stands for **Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support**. It's a distribution standard commonly written into trust documents to guide trustees on when and how they can distribute funds to beneficiaries. When a trust uses a HEMS standard, the trustee is authorized — but not required — to make distributions for a beneficiary's needs in these four categories.

HEMS is not a rigid formula. It's a flexible guideline that gives the trustee discretion while keeping distributions within reasonable bounds. The idea is to preserve trust assets for long-term benefit while ensuring beneficiaries can meet essential life needs.

## How HEMS Works

A trust document might say something like: *"The trustee may distribute income and principal for the beneficiary's health, education, maintenance, and support."* This means the trustee evaluates each distribution request through the HEMS lens.

The trustee should consider the beneficiary's **entire financial picture** — not just the trust. If a beneficiary has other income, savings, or resources, the trustee may reasonably decide that a distribution isn't necessary, even if the request falls loosely within one of the four categories.

## What Qualifies Under Each Category

### Health
- Medical, dental, and vision care
- Health insurance premiums
- Prescription medications
- Mental health treatment and counseling
- Rehabilitation or therapy programs
- Long-term care or assisted living expenses

### Education
- Tuition at accredited institutions
- Books, supplies, and required equipment
- Room and board while enrolled
- Vocational training and certification programs
- Graduate and professional school expenses
- Tutoring and test preparation

### Maintenance
- Basic living expenses such as housing and utilities
- Transportation and vehicle costs
- Insurance (home, auto, life)
- Clothing and household necessities
- Repairs to maintain a reasonable standard of living

### Support
- Expenses that sustain the beneficiary's accustomed lifestyle
- Childcare costs
- Professional development
- Reasonable recreation and quality-of-life expenses
- Support for dependents of the beneficiary

The line between maintenance and support can be blurry. **Maintenance** tends to cover the basics needed to keep a reasonable standard of living, while **support** can extend to maintaining a lifestyle the beneficiary was accustomed to before the trust existed.

## Documentation Requirements

Good documentation is essential when making HEMS distributions. The trustee should maintain records that show:

1. **The request** — What the beneficiary asked for, in writing if possible
2. **The purpose** — Which HEMS category the distribution falls under
3. **The reasoning** — Why the trustee approved or denied the request
4. **The beneficiary's circumstances** — Income, assets, and other resources the beneficiary has access to
5. **The distribution itself** — Amount, date, and how it was paid

## Common Misconceptions

- **HEMS does not mean the trustee must distribute.** It's permissive language — the trustee *may* distribute, not *shall*.
- **HEMS is not unlimited.** A beneficiary can't simply request any amount and expect it to be approved because it loosely relates to one of the four words.
- **HEMS doesn't require the beneficiary to be destitute.** The standard looks at maintaining a reasonable standard of living, not just covering bare necessities.
- **HEMS distributions can be challenged.** Other beneficiaries or interested parties may question distributions that appear unreasonable or unsupported.

## Why HEMS Matters

The HEMS standard exists to balance two competing goals: preserving trust assets for the future and ensuring beneficiaries have what they need in the present. It also provides a legal framework that protects the trustee. If a trustee makes a distribution that fits clearly within HEMS and is well-documented, that trustee has a strong defense against claims of mismanagement.
