# Trust Meeting Minutes

## Why Minutes Matter

Trust meeting minutes are the written record of decisions made during trust administration. They serve as proof that you, as trustee, are actively managing the trust, considering your duties, and making thoughtful decisions — not just winging it.

Minutes matter for three big reasons:

1. **Defensibility:** If a beneficiary ever challenges your decisions, minutes are your best evidence that you acted prudently and in good faith.
2. **Accountability:** Minutes create a paper trail that shows you're taking your responsibilities seriously.
3. **Continuity:** If something happens to you — illness, resignation, or death — your successor needs to understand what decisions were made and why.

Without minutes, it's your word against theirs. With minutes, you have a documented history of careful, considered administration.

## Types of Trust Meetings

Not every trust event requires formal minutes, but there are several situations where documentation is especially important:

### Annual Trust Reviews
At least once a year, the trustee should review the trust's overall status: investment performance, beneficiary circumstances, distribution history, and upcoming needs. This is a good time to document your assessment of the trust and any planned changes.

### Investment Decisions
When buying, selling, or rebalancing trust assets, document your reasoning. What information did you consider? What alternatives did you evaluate? Why did you choose this course of action?

### Distribution Decisions
Each distribution should be documented: who requested it, what it's for, how it fits the trust's distribution standard (HEMS, discretionary, etc.), and what analysis you performed.

### Beneficiary Communications
Significant conversations with beneficiaries — especially those involving requests, complaints, or disputes — should be documented while the details are fresh.

### Changes in Trust Administration
Hiring or firing professionals (attorneys, CPAs, investment advisors), changing trust strategies, or dealing with unexpected events like lawsuits or insurance claims all warrant documentation.

## What to Document

Effective trust minutes should include:

- **Date and time** of the meeting or decision
- **Who was present** — the trustee, co-trustees, advisors, or beneficiaries involved
- **Context** — what prompted the meeting or decision
- **Information reviewed** — reports, statements, advice, or other materials considered
- **Discussion summary** — the key points discussed, including alternatives considered
- **Decision** — what was decided and why
- **Action items** — who needs to do what next, and by when

## How Often to Document

There's no universal rule, but best practices suggest:

- **Annually:** A comprehensive trust review meeting, documented in full
- **As needed:** Any significant decision — distributions, investments, hiring, disputes
- **Quarterly:** If the trust is active with regular activity, quarterly reviews are advisable

Even if nothing notable happened, a brief entry noting that you reviewed the trust and found no changes needed is better than a gap in the record.

## Tips for Good Minutes

- **Write them promptly.** Don't wait weeks after a meeting. Document while it's fresh.
- **Be factual.** Record what happened and what was decided, not your personal feelings.
- **Be specific.** Include names, amounts, and dates rather than vague references.
- **Keep it organized.** Use consistent formatting so minutes are easy to find and review later.
- **Store them securely.** Minutes are trust records and should be kept with other trust documents.
- **Don't overthink it.** Minutes don't need to be lengthy or formal. A clear, concise record is better than no record at all.
